The Raw Force of Combat

Ares is perhaps the most controversial of the Olympians. While Athena represents the "chess game" of war, Ares represents the "blood and guts." He is the personification of the battle-cry, the clashing of bronze, and the terrifying adrenaline of the slaughter. Born to Zeus and Hera, he was often disliked by his father, who called him the most hateful god on Olympus. To the Greeks, Ares was a "necessary evil"—a god you prayed to so that he would stay away from your city, or so that he would possess your enemies with a paralyzing, mindless terror.


The Emotional Warrior

Unlike the stoic Athena, Ares is highly emotional and often impulsive. In the Iliad, he is frequently wounded and retreats to Olympus to complain to Zeus, showing that even the god of war is not invincible to pain or defeat. He is almost always accompanied by his sister Eris (Discord) and his sons, Phobos (Fear) and Deimos (Terror). However, Ares had a deeply protective side; he was the first god to be tried in a divine court (the Areopagus) for killing a son of Poseidon who had attempted to harm his daughter. This myth established him as a fierce protector of his children, a trait that humanized the otherwise violent deity.


The Armor and the Vulture

In art, Ares is the quintessential soldier, usually depicted with a gleaming helmet, a spear, and a shield. His sacred animals are the vulture and the dog—creatures that were often seen on the battlefields of the ancient world. He represents the "animal" instinct of survival and the overwhelming power of physical aggression. While the Greeks feared him, they also recognized that his energy was the driving force behind the defense of the home and the courage required to face death.
