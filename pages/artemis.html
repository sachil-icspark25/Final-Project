The Sovereign of the Wilderness

If Apollo represents the civilized city, his twin sister Artemis represents the "wilds" that exist outside it. Born just before her brother, she was said to have helped her mother Leto deliver Apollo, immediately becoming a patron of childbirth. Artemis famously asked Zeus for the gift of eternal maidenhood and a band of nymphs to accompany her. She is the "Lady of the Beasts," roaming the mountains and forests with a silver bow, protecting young animals and maintaining the balance of nature.


The Protector and the Punisher

Artemis is a fierce protector of her boundaries and her dignity. The most famous example of her wrath is the myth of Actaeon, a hunter who accidentally saw her bathing. She transformed him into a stag, and he was torn apart by his own hounds. This story highlights the "untouchable" nature of the wilderness; nature is beautiful, but it is not for human voyeurism or exploitation. However, she was also the "Protector of the Young." Every Greek girl, before marriage, would dedicate her toys and a lock of hair to Artemis, asking for protection during the dangerous transition into womanhood.


The Moon and the Night

As her brother moved the sun, Artemis eventually became identified with Selene, the Moon. She represents the "cool" light of the night, a contrast to Apollo’s "hot" solar energy. She is often depicted in a short hunting tunic with a quiver of arrows, accompanied by a stag or a hunting dog. Artemis reminds us that there is a part of the human spirit that belongs to the woods—a part that is independent, fierce, and fundamentally wild.
